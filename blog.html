Perspective on the development of science

Huu-Tien Dang

Science does not progress in a straight line. It moves in bursts — long periods of quiet accumulation interrupted by moments where everything shifts. What looks like a settled understanding can, overnight, become a footnote. The history of physics, biology, and computation all tell the same story: the most important advances often come not from answering the questions everyone is asking, but from questioning whether those questions were the right ones in the first place.

At the heart of science is a simple motivation:

Scientific research is an activity in which we, motivated by intellectual curiosity and a desire for exploration, seek to understand truths about various phenomena surrounding us, how they happen, what causes them to happen.

This curiosity-first framing matters. It pushes back against a purely utilitarian view of research — the idea that science is valuable only insofar as it produces immediate, applicable results. Some of the most consequential discoveries began with people who had no idea what their findings would eventually be used for. Faraday's work on electromagnetism, Mendel's pea plants, Shannon's mathematical theory of communication — none of these were pursued because their authors saw a product roadmap at the end. They were pursued because the phenomena were interesting and the questions were open.

That said, curiosity alone is not enough. Science is also a social enterprise, shaped by institutions, incentive structures, and the collective memory encoded in papers, textbooks, and mentorship. A lone genius with a great idea still needs a community willing to engage critically with that idea, to test it, challenge it, and build on it. The development of science is therefore as much about culture as it is about individual insight — about creating environments where intellectual risk-taking is rewarded and where being wrong is understood as a necessary step, not a failure.

Looking at the current moment in machine learning and AI research, I notice both sides of this tension. On one hand, there is an extraordinary amount of curiosity and energy — more people working on fundamental questions about learning, reasoning, and representation than at any previous point in history. On the other hand, the field is also under enormous pressure to deliver, to scale, to ship. That pressure can compress the space between observation and conclusion, between finding and understanding.

The challenge, then, is to preserve the exploratory spirit even as the stakes get higher. To ask not just what works but why it works, and to remain genuinely open to answers that complicate or overturn what we thought we knew. That, I think, is what it means to take science seriously — not as a set of facts to be accumulated, but as a practice of honest inquiry.
